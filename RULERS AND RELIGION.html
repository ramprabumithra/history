INDIA

Chandragupta Maurya was one of the most important rulers in the history of India who is credited with unifying small independent states in India to form a large single kingdom under one administration.Chandragupta Maurya was born in 340 BC in Bihar, and was guided by Chanaka, a great Brahmin economics and political science scholar, who later became his mentor.


The spread of Buddhism in many parts of the world is credited to Emperor Ashoka who was the third ruler of the Mauryan Empire. He was also known as ‘Ashoka the Great’ as he ruled almost the entire Indian subcontinent. He focused on expanding his empire continuously. His reign is considered one of India’s most glorious periods.


Emperor Krishnadevaraya had a great track record of brilliant achievements and was able to maintain political stability. His success is often compared to that of the greatest emperors of Asia and Europe.

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj established a royal name for himself in history with his administrative skills by upholding the Swarajya values and the Maratha heritage. He was known for his bravery and tactics with which he won numerous wars against the Mughals.


Under his reign the first diplomatic relations between China and India were established. The economy also thrived, and the capital at Kannauj became a great centre of trade. King Harshavardhana can be said to have been a humane emperor. Underprivileged citizens had access to rest- houses which the emperor built, complete with food, drink and medicine. He stayed in touch with his people’s state by travelling across his kingdom to observe the situation.


India is home to the world's oldest religions, Hinduism and Buddhism, as well as Jainism. All three evolved from shared beliefs and traditions, such as reincarnation, karma, and liberation and achieving nirvana. These beliefs and traditions evolved in the Indus River Valley around 3500 BCE

EGYPT

Ramesses II was the third pharaoh of the Nineteenth Dynasty of Egypt. He is often regarded as the greatest, most celebrated, and most powerful pharaoh of the New Kingdom, itself the most powerful period of Ancient Egypt.


Amenhotep III, also called Amenophis III, king of ancient Egypt (reigned 1390–53 BCE) in a period of peaceful prosperity, who devoted himself to expanding diplomatic contacts and to extensive building in Egypt and Nubia.


Hatshepsut re-established the trade networks that had been disrupted,was one of the most prolific builders in Ancient Egypt, commissioning hundreds of construction projects throughout both Upper Egypt and Lower Egypt.


Cleopatra was directly involved in the administrative affairs of her domain, tackling crises such as famine by ordering royal granaries to distribute food to the starving populace during a drought at the beginning of her reign.She was also multilingual – historical accounts report her speaking between 5 and 9 languages, including her native Greek, Egyptian, Arabic and Hebrew.


The religion of Ancient Egypt lasted for more than 3,000 years, and was polytheistic, meaning there were a multitude of deities, who were believed to reside within and control the forces of nature.


Narmer was an ancient Egyptian pharaoh of the Early Dynastic Period. He was the successor to the Protodynastic king Ka. Many scholars consider him the unifier of Egypt and founder of the First Dynasty, and in turn the first king of a unified Egypt.

ROME

Caesar Augustus, also known as Octavian, was the first Roman emperor, reigning from 27 BC until his death in AD 14. His status as the founder of the Roman Principate has consolidated a legacy as one of the most effective leaders in human history.


Julius Caesar was a good leader even after he became Roman dictator. Before he became all powerful, Caesar revealed himself to have extraordinary leadership capabilities. He was charismatic, able to bend those around him to his will, and an excellent orator. He was a brilliant military strategist and a bold risk-taker.


Although the Emperor Hadrian spent little time in Rome itself, he left his mark on the imperial capital in the form of art and architecture. Arguably his most famous achievement was the Pantheon, which he likely completed by 125 CE. It is made of brick and concrete and once displayed a marble façade


Marcus Aurelius was the last of the Five Good Emperors of Rome. His reign (161–180 CE) marked the end of a period of internal tranquility and good government. After his death the empire quickly descended into civil war. He has symbolized the Golden Age of the Roman Empire for many generations in the West.


As emperor, Trajan expanded the Roman Empire to become larger than ever before. He conquered Dacia (now part of Romania), which provided land for Roman settlers and rich pickings from gold and salt mines. He then attacked the Parthians, Rome's old enemy in the East, who lived in what is now part of Iran.


The Roman Empire was a primarily polytheistic civilization, which meant that people recognized and worshiped multiple gods and goddesses. Despite the presence of monotheistic religions within the empire, such as Judaism and early Christianity, Romans honored multiple deities.

BRITAIN

Known as "the Merry Monarch," Charles II was king of Great Britain and Ireland from 1660 to 1685. His political adaptability enabled him to guide hiscountry through the religious unrest between Anglicans, Catholics, and dissenters that came to signify much of his reign.


Richard I – aka Richard the Lionheart – is remembered for being a chivalrous medieval king; for battling Saladin during the Crusades; and for rebelling against his father, Henry II (1133–89).


Queen Victoria. Under her reign, England and the British Empire achieved the pinnacle of its power. Victorian England possessed the largest empire by land area and global influence that has ever existed. Although by her time the monarchy’s influence on government was greatly curtailed, her greatness lies in the British monarchy’s ability to hand power over to the people and an aggressively Imperialistic overseas army and navy.


William I the Conqueror. Conquered England at Hastings, one of the most important battles in world history, forced the country to unite as one, reorganized the administration to ensure a more efficient use of taxation, and laid the foundation of government and law for England that would provide the building block for its future global empire.


In 1500 England was a Roman Catholic country. By 1750, after the turmoil of the 16th century Reformation and the 17th century civil wars , Britain was the leading Protestant power in Europe.


Queen Elizabeth II has been extremely popular for nearly all of her long reign, is known for taking a serious interest in government and political affairs apart from her ceremonial duties, and is credited with modernizing many aspects of the monarchy.
